Alkane Structural Isomer Search Toy Problem

Problem Statement

Given a set of $N$ carbon atoms and a candidate set of $M$ possible carbon-carbon bonds, the task is to select a subset of bonds that forms a valid alkane carbon skeleton. Validity requires that each carbon atom attains a specified valence (degree), the total number of selected bonds equals $N-1$ (ensuring a tree structure), and the selected bonds connect all carbon atoms into a single component. The objective is to find any feasible bond configuration, which corresponds to minimizing a constant objective function subject to these structural constraints.

Decision Variables

Let $\mathcal{B} = \{1, \dots, M\}$ denote the set of candidate bonds. For each candidate bond $j \in \mathcal{B}$, introduce a binary decision variable $x_j \in \{0, 1\}$, where $x_j = 1$ indicates that bond $j$ is selected in the skeleton, and $x_j = 0$ otherwise. Each bond $j$ connects exactly two carbon atoms, denoted $u_j, v_j \in \{1, \dots, N\}$. Let $I_i \subseteq \mathcal{B}$ be the set of candidate bonds incident to atom $i$, and let $d_i$ denote the required degree for atom $i$.

QUBO Derivation

Step 1 --- Objective. The problem is a pure feasibility problem with a constant objective function. In QUBO minimization form, this is expressed as: \begin{align} H_{\text{obj}}(\mathbf{x}) = 0. \end{align} Step 2 --- Constraints. The structural requirements are written as equality constraints: \begin{align} \sum_{j \in I_i} x_j &= d_i, \quad \forall i \in \{1, \dots, N\}, \tag{Degree} \\ \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}} x_j &= N - 1. \tag{Bond Count} \end{align} The connectivity constraint requires that the subgraph induced by $\{j \in \mathcal{B} : x_j = 1\}$ is connected. For tree structures on $N$ vertices, the degree and bond count constraints are often sufficient to enforce connectivity in small instances; we therefore focus the penalty formulation on the degree and bond count constraints. Step 3 --- Penalty Expansion. We convert each equality constraint to a quadratic penalty with weights $\lambda_D > 0$ and $\lambda_B > 0$. (a) Squared penalty form: \begin{align} H_D(\mathbf{x}) &= \lambda_D \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \sum_{j \in I_i} x_j - d_i \right)^2, \\ H_B(\mathbf{x}) &= \lambda_B \left( \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}} x_j - (N-1) \right)^2. \end{align} (b) Full expansion using $x_j^2 = x_j$: \begin{align} H_D(\mathbf{x}) &= \lambda_D \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \sum_{j \in I_i} x_j^2 + \sum_{\substack{j,k \in I_i \\ j \neq k}} x_j x_k - 2d_i \sum_{j \in I_i} x_j + d_i^2 \right) \nonumber \\ &= \lambda_D \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \sum_{j \in I_i} (1 - 2d_i) x_j + \sum_{\substack{j,k \in I_i \\ j \neq k}} x_j x_k + d_i^2 \right), \\ H_B(\mathbf{x}) &= \lambda_B \left( \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}} x_j^2 + \sum_{\substack{j,k \in \mathcal{B} \\ j \neq k}} x_j x_k - 2(N-1)\sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}} x_j + (N-1)^2 \right) \nonumber \\ &= \lambda_B \left( \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}} (1 - 2(N-1)) x_j + \sum_{\substack{j,k \in \mathcal{B} \\ j \neq k}} x_j x_k + (N-1)^2 \right). \end{align} (c) General penalty contribution to $H$: Since each bond $j$ is incident to exactly two atoms $u_j$ and $v_j$, the quadratic term $\sum_{\substack{j,k \in I_i \\ j \neq k}} x_j x_k$ appears in the sum for atom $i$ only when both $j$ and $k$ share atom $i$. Thus, for any pair $j \neq k$, the coefficient of $x_j x_k$ in $H_D$ is $2\lambda_D$ if $j$ and $k$ share an atom, and $0$ otherwise. The linear coefficient for $x_j$ in $H_D$ is $\lambda_D \left( (1 - 2d_{u_j}) + (1 - 2d_{v_j}) \right) = \lambda_D (2 - 2d_{u_j} - 2d_{v_j})$. The linear coefficient for $x_j$ in $H_B$ is $\lambda_B (1 - 2(N-1)) = \lambda_B (3 - 2N)$. Step 4 --- Combined Hamiltonian. \begin{align} H(\mathbf{x}) &= H_{\text{obj}}(\mathbf{x}) + H_D(\mathbf{x}) + H_B(\mathbf{x}) \nonumber \\ &= \sum_{j \in \mathcal{B}} \left[ \lambda_D (2 - 2d_{u_j} - 2d_{v_j}) + \lambda_B (3 - 2N) \right] x_j \nonumber \\ &\quad + \sum_{j < k} \left[ 2\lambda_D \cdot \mathbb{I}(\text{atoms}(j) \cap \text{atoms}(k) \neq \emptyset) + 2\lambda_B \right] x_j x_k + c, \end{align} where $c = \lambda_D \sum_{i=1}^N d_i^2 + \lambda_B (N-1)^2$. Reading off the coefficients from the expanded Hamiltonian yields the general closed-form expressions: \begin{align} Q_{j,j} &= \lambda_D (2 - 2d_{u_j} - 2d_{v_j}) + \lambda_B (3 - 2N), \quad \forall j \in \mathcal{B}, \\ Q_{j,k} &= 2\lambda_D \cdot \mathbb{I}(\text{atoms}(j) \cap \text{atoms}(k) \neq \emptyset) + 2\lambda_B, \quad \forall j \neq k, \\ c &= \lambda_D \sum_{i=1}^N d_i^2 + \lambda_B (N-1)^2. \end{align}

Penalty Weight Justification

The penalty weights $\lambda_D$ and $\lambda_B$ must be chosen sufficiently large to ensure that any constraint violation increases the energy above the minimum feasible value. Since the objective function is constant ($0$), the energy of any feasible solution equals the offset $c$. Violating any equality constraint by the minimum integer amount of $1$ produces a squared penalty of at least $1$. Therefore, selecting $\lambda_D, \lambda_B > 0$ guarantees that any infeasible configuration incurs a strictly positive penalty, yielding $H(\mathbf{x}) > c$. In practice, a uniform weight $\lambda = 100$ is chosen to provide a robust margin against numerical noise and to dominate any potential objective fluctuations in extended formulations.

Correctness Argument

Minimizing $H(\mathbf{x})$ over $\mathbf{x} \in \{0,1\}^M$ recovers the optimal solution because the penalty terms are non-negative and vanish exactly when all constraints are satisfied. Consequently, the energy of any feasible solution equals the constant offset $c$, while any infeasible solution incurs a strictly positive penalty, yielding $H(\mathbf{x}) > c$. Therefore, the global minimum of $H$ must occur at a feasible configuration. Since the objective function is constant, all feasible configurations are optimal, and the QUBO minimization correctly identifies the set of valid alkane skeletons.
